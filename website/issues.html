Plan Review — Open Issues & Principle-Violations

Companion to plan.html. Living open-issues register — first compiled 2026-06-02, last updated 2026-06-03.

This is the living, single source of truth for open issues in website/plan.html — not a frozen snapshot. It began as the verbatim issue list from the plan review and is now kept by one rule: anything we confirm gets documented here; anything we fix in the plan gets removed. Each entry follows one shape: why the planner reached for it (it almost always had a good reason), and why it may nonetheless be an issue — including the ones that look perfectly legitimate but quietly violate a stated principle. These are arguments to have and bugs to consider, recorded for the operator's decision.

The lineup

Fair. Here it is as a rogues' gallery — each one: why the planner reached for it, why I'd squint at it.

First, three I wrongly accused (the full read cleared them): the --bind-all-interfaces flag — you asked for it, charges dropped. The fast/hybrid/slow generation modes — hardcoded per template, never a menu, the planner behaved. The dropped bounding box — logged and counted, not snuck under the rug.

Now the lineup.

1. The one-strike prompt. Lock the prompt for the whole session and, the instant LocateAnything-3B (NVIDIA's 3-billion-parameter grounding model) sneers at it, auto-release ownership — clean, atomic, no half-claimed states. But one typo the model dislikes ejects you from the streamer's chair, on a demo whose entire sport is fiddling with prompts. Cruel.
2. The clock bouncer. Refuse to boot if the wall clock reads before 2024, because Transport Layer Security can't validate a certificate against a 1970 clock — honest failure. But a "sovereign, never-goes-down" box that won't start until Network Time Protocol whispers it the time is a small external dependency wearing a large independence badge.
3. The 30-second velvet rope. When the streamer drops, hold their slot for 30 seconds so an iPhone hopping Wi-Fi to cellular can stroll back in. But you said "if anybody disconnects, anybody can take over" — and for half a minute, nobody can. A defensible reading of "cleanly." Still a reading.
4. Collective punishment. Streamer vanished, so tear down every viewer at once — you can't watch a ghost. But a three-second streamer hiccup therefore blacks out the whole audience, who all reconnect-storm in unison. One flaky phone, everyone's screen.
5. The Internet-Protocol-version-4 doorman. You named 127.0.0.1 and 0.0.0.0, neither is version 6, so: IPv4 only, no IPv6 socket, ever. But that reads "two example addresses" as "outlaw the other protocol," and silently bars every viewer stranded on an IPv6-only phone behind Carrier-Grade Network Address Translation. For "everybody viewing," that's a real somebody.
6. The blurry star of the show. Cap capture at 1280×720 because the forced H.264 Constrained Baseline Level 3.1 codec can't legally climb higher. But we then hand the grounding model a deliberately shrunk frame — against its stated appetite for 1440p — and paint its answers a full second late over buttery video. The detection is the act; we dimmed the spotlight to make the curtains sway nicely.
7. The unfair referee. If LocateAnything-3B blips, fast-drain the streamer — resuming into a dead model is pointless. But the streamer's own network blip earns a cozy 30-second grace, while the model's hiccup — not their fault in the slightest — ejects them on the spot. Punished hardest for the one thing they didn't do.
8. One waiter, whole restaurant. Run the entire Selective Forwarding Unit on a single actor task — no locks, no shared-state races, gloriously correct. But "everybody viewing" then squeezes through one thread sized for ~50 guests, and the plan itself shrugs that 100 is untested. Correct, right up until it's a queue.

— the correctness gremlins —

9. The portrait trap. Clamp capture to 1280 wide, 720 tall, and reject anything taller than 720 or skinnier than 640 as out-of-spec. But hold a phone upright and you get 720×1280 — identical macroblock budget, yet it trips the "too tall" wire and gets bounced. We may be slamming the door on every streamer who didn't turn their phone sideways. Worth confirming before it ships.
10. The untested heart valve. The automated test fabricates the bounding-box WebRTC DataChannel by hand, so the pipeline glows green. But that's the exact shortcut that hides the bug we already bled from — the one where the browser, not the server, must offer that channel. The plan literally says a real client "would have caught it." So use one.

— the petty offenders —

11. The mode that never runs. "Fast" generation mode is defined, demanded at startup, and chosen by exactly zero templates — the server refuses to boot over a capability it never once uses.
12. The 16,384-character ghost. A maximum-prompt-length constant nobody enforces (the frontend stops at 200, the server validates nothing) — the same dead-constant smell the plan proudly swept out elsewhere.
13. One key for the whole kingdom. The secret that lets you watch is the secret that lets you seize the camera. Charming when everyone with the link is a friend; spicy the moment the link wanders.
If you only stare hard at four: #1 and #4 are behaviors I'd want you to re-decide on purpose; #9 and #10 are the correctness bugs I'd chase before anything reaches a real phone.

Deeper cuts

Oh, there's more — these are the ones hiding behind a sensible-sounding sentence. Same gallery, deeper cuts.

The unpinned codec. "Pin all versions!" you said, and the planner obeyed beautifully — every Rust crate to the digit, every Docker base image to its Secure Hash Algorithm 256 fingerprint. Then left the Advanced Package Tool (APT) packages floating, "to absorb Debian security updates." So the one library actually decoding your video — libavcodec and its siblings — is whatever the Debian Trixie mirror serves the morning you rebuild. The pinned base-image digest doesn't save you; apt-get install reaches right past it. You asked for reproducible; this is reproducible-except-the-part-that-touches-the-pixels.
The colorspace nobody measured. libswscale defaults to BT.601, webcams mostly mean BT.709, so the plan reaches through as_mut_ptr() and forces BT.709 limited→full — explicit, rigorous-sounding, it even shows its work. But it forces 709 blind: it never reads what the stream actually signaled, so a genuine BT.601 camera gets a silently-wrong matrix feeding the detector — the exact silent-wrong-in-the-detection-path the project forbids. (The unwritten helper hides a second trap: for RGB output libswscale ignores dstRange and keys full-range off srcRange, so "full destination range" is misleading.) The kicker: the signal isn't lost — the H.264 VUI rides the bitstream, libavcodec parses it onto the frame, and ffmpeg-next exposes color_space() / color_range(). We're guessing at a number the decoder already knows.
The bouncer with an infinite guest book. The home-grown Session Traversal Utilities for Network Address Translation (STUN) server rate-limits per source address and sweeps its table every 60 seconds — tidy, sovereign, no amplification. But it's an unauthenticated public User Datagram Protocol port, and it files one entry per distinct source address until the next sweep. Spray it with spoofed sources and the table balloons for a full minute before the broom comes. The plan itself calls this bound "soft" — on the single most exposed surface in the system. Soft is not a word you want there.
Three nurses, one pulse. You said deciding whether a user is still connected is sometimes "none of our business." The plan then checks each viewer's liveness three ways at once: an application-level keepalive every 15 seconds, a Transmission Control Protocol keepalive at ~25, and Interactive Connectivity Establishment consent-freshness at ~21.5. Each is defensible as zombie-task hygiene — but it's three overlapping mechanisms diligently answering the exact question you were wary of asking.
The graceful exit nobody takes. The plan choreographs a lovely 8-second farewell: send every peer a DataChannel goodbye, flush it, then disconnect — so browsers see a clean handoff instead of a frozen frame. And then it names the Out-Of-Memory killer "the loud-failure signal we want." But the Out-Of-Memory killer is SIGKILL — it shoots the process mid-word, skips the entire farewell, and leaves every viewer frozen until consent-freshness expires ~21 seconds later. The safety net under the ballet is a trapdoor.
No relay, no entry. Sovereign to the bone, the plan runs its own STUN and defers a Traversal Using Relays around Network Address Translation (TURN) server until failures cross 2 percent. But "everybody viewing" includes phones on captive or corporate Wi-Fi that block User Datagram Protocol entirely — and with no relay over Transmission Control Protocol port 443 to fall back to, those people don't get a stuttery stream. They get a spinner, forever, with no error. The most fire-and-forget failure in the document.
The test in a parallel universe. The lone automated end-to-end test runs on a single-threaded runtime; production runs four worker threads. So the entire careful argument that two people can't grab the streamer slot at once — "no .await between checking ownership and setting it" — is proven on paper and then rehearsed on the one runtime where the race it defends against literally cannot happen.
A design system for a one-camera demo. You asked for an interface "friendly for mobile and Desktop." The plan replied with roughly fifteen subsections of viewport choreography: dynamic-viewport-height units, safe-area insets for the iPhone notch, a heads-up display that auto-hides and breathes (unless you prefer reduced motion), three blessed scroll regions, a full light-and-dark palette. It's gorgeous couture — for one streamer and a grid of seven cards.
The biennial tap on the shoulder. Apple's Security framework hard-caps a certificate at 825 days, so the plan mints an 824-day one and asks the operator to regenerate "every ~27 months." It honors your "don't let a certificate quietly kill it" wish as far as Apple physically permits — yet a box you wanted to forget about still taps you on the shoulder every two years. (This one's Apple's fault, not the planner's — but it's the residue of the very fear you named.)
The one panic the no-panic rule allows. Your hard rule is no unwrap, expect, or panic in runtime code, honored everywhere but one spot the plan names aloud: mozjpeg's libjpeg core uses setjmp/longjmp, which surfaces as a Rust panic, and the plan deliberately does not catch_unwind it — "if a panic occurs, --restart unless-stopped recovers." It argues the panic is unreachable because libswscale always hands it a valid buffer. Probably true — but it's an assumption guarding a live panic on the bounding-box hot path, in a design whose whole premise is that panics don't happen. Worth recording so "unreachable" is a claim someone signs, not a silent reliance.
The healthy viewer culled for going quiet. A viewer is disconnected when its signaling keepalive is silent for 30 seconds — but its video runs on a separate User Datagram Protocol path with its own liveness (Interactive Connectivity Establishment consent freshness, ~21.5 seconds). A viewer whose signaling WebSocket degrades while its video keeps arriving gets evicted mid-watch: the server has direct evidence the peer is alive — consent fresh, packets flowing — yet trusts the quieter signal and kills it. It self-heals (the reconnect loop re-attaches), so it's a transient hiccup, not a hard failure — but it is a genuine liveness false-positive. (Adjacent to "Three nurses," but that one flags having three redundant checks; this flags one of them being wrong.)
The contract behind two padlocks. The demo keeps its own copy of LocateAnything's prompt contract in the protocol crate — the seven template strings, the expected reference URL, the expected generation modes — guarded by two hard-fail tripwires: a compile-time byte-equality test against the sibling prompts.py, and a boot-time capabilities cross-check. Mostly sensible, since the model doesn't expose the templates as labeled fields. The one needless edge: the boot tripwire keys its hard-fail on an exact URL string — the most mutable of its four checks. Rename the repo or its branch and the demo refuses to boot until you edit a constant in a different project, even though it verifies the stable model name one line up. (It never fetches that URL — GitHub being down is irrelevant — it only string-matches it.) Key the hard-fail on model plus modes; demote the URL to a warning.
"Survive restart" — except who's holding the camera. The certificate and clock survive a restart on disk, as required. Ownership does not: the streamer slot and its session id live only in memory, so a crash, an update, or the by-design Out-Of-Memory restart re-initializes to the idle state and the active streamer loses the slot. They reconnect, get a clean "session expired," and fall back to the prompt grid — loud and tidy. But your verbatim ask was that the whole setup "absolutely survive restart, and be just as correct." Reverting to idle after a crash (nobody is actually live) is arguably the right reading — but it's a real boundary on a verbatim requirement, and under perfect correctness it deserves an explicit yes rather than an implicit one.
If I had to point at the two that are genuinely dangerous rather than merely philosophical: the infinite guest book (a remote stranger can bloat memory until the Out-Of-Memory killer restarts you — and #4 means that restart is ugly) and the unpinned codec (your "pin everything" rule has a hole exactly where the video flows). The rest are arguments worth having; those two are bugs worth closing.
